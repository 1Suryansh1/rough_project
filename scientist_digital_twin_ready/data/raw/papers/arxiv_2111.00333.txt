In this contribution we report about Feynman‚Äôs approach to gravitation, starting from
the records of his interventions at the Chapel Hill Conference of 1957. As well known,
Feynman was concerned about the relation of gravitation with the rest of physics. Proba-
bly for this reason, he promoted an unusual, Ô¨Åeld theoretical approach to general relativ-
ity, in which, after the recognition that the interaction must be mediated by the quanta of
a massless spin-2 Ô¨Åeld, Einstein‚Äôs Ô¨Åeld equations should follow from the general properties
of Lorentz-invariant quantum Ô¨Åeld theory, plus self-consistency requirements. Quantum
corrections would then be included by considering loop diagrams. These ideas were fur-
ther developed by Feynman in his famous lectures on gravitation, delivered at Caltech
in 1962-63, and in a handful of published papers, where he also introduced some Ô¨Åeld
theoretical tools which were soon recognized to be of general interest, such as ghosts and
the tree theorem. Some original pieces of Feynman‚Äôs work on gravity are also present
in a set of unpublished lectures delivered at Hughes Aircraft Company in 1966-67 and
devoted primarily to astrophysics and cosmology. Some comments on the relation be-
tween Feynman‚Äôs approach to gravity and his ideas on the quantum foundations of the
fundamental interactions are included.
Keywords: Gravitation; Quantum Ô¨Åeld theory; Loop diagrams.

3. Fighting with loops: the renormalization of gravity
After obtaining the Einstein-Hilbert action and the full nonlinear Einstein gravi-
tational Ô¨Åeld equations, Feynman‚Äôs eÔ¨Äorts were mainly directed towards discussing
quantum Ô¨Åeld theory issues beyond the tree level approximation, i.e. loop diagrams,
unitarity and renormalization.
To the best of our knowledge, these issues were publicly addressed for the Ô¨Årst
time by Feynman in 1961 at the already mentioned La Jolla conference9, where
nonlinearity was recognized as the very source of diÔ¨Éculty within both gravitation
and Yang-Mills theories. Indeed the sources of the gravitational Ô¨Åeld are energy
and momentum, and the gravitational Ô¨Åeld carries energy and momentum itself. In
the same way, the source of a Yang-Mills Ô¨Åeld is the isotopic spin current, and the
Yang-Mills Ô¨Åeld carries isotopic spin itself. This means that both the gravitational
Ô¨Åeld and the Yang-Mills Ô¨Åeld are self-coupled, resulting in nonlinear Ô¨Åeld theories.
A diÔ¨Éculty that nonlinearity brings about is the fact that loop diagrams seem to
clash with unitarity.

cially coupled to the external Ô¨Åeld. In this way both unitarity and gauge invariance

4. Concluding remarks
In this paper we focused on Feynman‚Äôs contributions to the research in quantum
gravity, starting from his interventions at Chapel Hill conference in 1957 and ending
with the Wheeler festschrift papers. His approach was Ô¨Åeld theoretical rather than
geometric, reÔ¨Çecting his strong belief in the unity of Nature, which is quantum
at the deepest level. Quantization of gravity, according to him, simply had to be
